Fostering Social Development

Social development encompasses the ability to form relationships, cooperate and empathise with others. Have you ever seen how quickly toddlers copy each other’s smiles and actions? Social learning starts with simple interactions, like a baby cooing back to a caregiver or two toddlers giggling over the same toy. These moments build trust, relationships and a sense of belonging [2]. Educators nurture this growth through warm, responsive care: “serve and return” exchanges— smiling, echoing a baby’s sound or engaging in peek‑a‑boo—help build both social and brain pathways. For toddlers, modelling sharing and guiding turn‑taking during play is essential [8]. Inclusive environments that reflect children’s cultures act as mirrors and windows, affirming identities and encouraging cooperation [7].

Teaching Competencies & Skills

Competency Description
Serve & Return Interaction Offer warm, reciprocal exchanges; respond promptly to cues; imitate coos and facial expressions; model eye contact and attentive listening [2].
Modelling & Coaching Model sharing and guide turn‑taking during play; describe feelings and social situations; encourage empathy through gentle language [8].
Cultural Inclusion Incorporate children’s home languages, songs and traditions; display diverse family photographs [7].
Facilitating Peer Interaction Arrange small‑group activities; scaffold conflict resolution; encourage cooperative problem solving.

Authentic Curriculum Provision

Social learning happens through play with caregivers and peers. Sing call‑and‑response songs (music); engage in hand‑clapping rhymes (movement); use puppet shows to model friendship scenarios (drama); read books featuring cooperation and diversity (language); and provide group art projects such as collaborative murals (art).

Learning Opportunities

Age Group Activity Description & Curriculum Link
0–12 months Mirror Play Sit with an infant in front of a mirror and copy each other’s faces and smiles. Builds trust, introduces turn‑taking and nurtures connection. Links: Movement & Music.
12–24 months Sharing Baskets Offer a basket of soft toys or objects. Encourage toddlers to choose an item and hand it to a friend, practising giving and sharing. Links: Humanities & Social Sciences.
2–3 years Friend Circle Dance Toddlers hold hands in a circle and move to music, stopping together on cue. Promotes cooperation and group awareness. Links: Art and Drama.

Books, Songs, Rhymes & Movement Game

Use these resources to nurture social skills. Record videos featuring different group members.

Children’s Books

  • Rosie is My Best Friend by Ali Pye – celebrates teamwork and play, showing how friendship makes everyday moments joyful. Ask, “What makes someone a good friend like Rosie?” after reading.
  • Little Unicorn is Sad by Aurélie Chien Chow Chine – teaches children to recognise and manage feelings, showing friends offering support. Discuss how to help a friend who feels sad.

Songs

  • “The More We Get Together” – encourages group connection and highlights happiness in shared experiences.
  • “If You’re Happy and You Know It” – uses actions and emotions to help toddlers express feelings alongside peers.

Rhymes/Lullabies

  • “Pat‑a‑Cake” – promotes cooperative play and hand‑to‑hand interaction, ideal for small groups.
  • “Row, Row, Row Your Boat” – supports pair play, teaching timing and gentle coordination.

Movement Game

Ring Around the Rosie invites toddlers to hold hands, walk in a circle and fall down together. This playful game develops group cooperation, turn‑taking and social timing.
