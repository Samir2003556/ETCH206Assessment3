Supporting Cognitive Development

Cognitive development of infants and toddlers forms a basis to the processes of problem solving, learning, and thinking. This is the stage of rapid brain development when children learn key competencies including the ability to reason, remember and be attentive (Berk & Meyers, 2023). The sensorimotor stage is a phase expressed by Piaget theory, where the infants discover things by using sense and movement in the environment (McDevitt & Ormrod, 2020). The sociocultural theory underlines the aspect of social interaction as the cause of enhancing cognitive abilities as put forward by Vygotsky (1978).

Teaching Competencies & Skills

Competency Description
Observation & Assessment Watch how infants explore objects, notice emerging problem‑solving strategies and use this information to plan next steps.
Scaffolding Provide just‑enough assistance to help children achieve a task without taking over; gradually reduce support as they gain mastery [4].
Language & Narration Describe what the child is doing, label objects and actions, and introduce new words to support thinking.
Provide Novel Challenges Rotate materials to maintain curiosity; present challenges slightly above current ability so children stretch their thinking.
Encourage Exploration Create safe spaces for infants to manipulate objects, make predictions and see outcomes; avoid over‑structuring.

Authentic Curriculum Provision

Cognitive growth flourishes when learning opportunities are embedded within everyday routines and play. Integrate multiple curriculum areas: mathematics through problem‑solving, reasoning, classification and patterning; science through inquiry, observation and simple experimentation; English through storytelling, oral language and emergent literacy; and Humanities and Social Sciences through children’s questions about their world. Use treasure baskets filled with household objects to explore textures (science); offer stacking blocks, shape sorters and construction toys (mathematics & engineering); read engaging picture books and sing counting songs (language & music); and provide opportunities for cause‑and‑effect exploration with water, sand and movement games.

Learning & Wellbeing Outcomes

Cognitive learning is intertwined with wellbeing. Shared routines and rituals support executive functioning, including memory and attention. Helping children regulate their emotions enhances focused thinking and problem solving. Engaging with books, patterns and technology fosters abstract reasoning and literacy‑based cognition.

Learning Opportunities

Age Group Activity Description & Curriculum Link
0–12 months Sensory Exploration Play Provide a variety of objects with different textures, colours and sounds (spoons, fabric, rattles). Let infants explore freely while you narrate their discoveries. Links: Science, Art.
12–24 months Simple Puzzles & Construction Toys Offer large wooden puzzles and basic construction sets. Encourage toddlers to problem solve, match shapes and build towers. Links: Mathematics, Engineering.
2–3 years Science & Discovery Exploration Set up simple science experiments such as water play with floating and sinking objects or magnifying glasses to examine leaves. Encourage prediction, observation and discussion. Links: Science, Mathematics, Humanities.

Books, Songs, Rhymes & Movement Game

Choose from the following resources to support cognitive growth. Record a video of each book reading, song, rhyme and game featuring different group members.

Children’s Books

  • Five Big Trucks – builds memory, recognition and problem‑solving skills; counting and sequencing concepts support early numeracy.
  • The Little Rabbit Who Lost Her Hop – follows a sequence of events that sharpens memory and recall; encourages symbolic thinking.

Songs

  • “The Wheels on the Bus” – fosters memory, sequencing and language development through repetitive, predictable patterns.
  • “Head, Shoulders, Knees and Toes” – encourages following multi‑step instructions and improves memory, listening skills and executive function.

Rhymes/Lullabies

  • “Make a Pizza” – supports cognitive skills through sequencing and cause‑and‑effect; children associate actions with steps in the rhyme.
  • “Five Little Ducks” – repetition and counting help children anticipate events, strengthening memory and recall.

Movement Game

Simon Says – encourages self‑management and problem‑solving skills. Children listen carefully and follow directions only when preceded by “Simon says,” practising attention and impulse control.
